## What this process is for

Forecast Financials keeps the financial outlook for active work up to date. It gives teams and portfolio leaders a reliable view of where costs are heading so they can act before problems become surprises.

This is not a once-and-done budget exercise. It is a recurring discipline that runs throughout delivery and into live operation.

## When to use it

- When an approved investment moves into active delivery
- At regular intervals during delivery, typically monthly or per sprint cycle
- When scope, timeline or cost assumptions change materially
- When portfolio-level forecasts are being consolidated
- When a product moves into live operation and ongoing cost tracking begins

This process is most active during the Execution and Operation and Evaluation financial stages. It connects to the Develop and Deliver, Deploy, and Operate and Monitor lifecycle stages.

## Key questions to answer

- Are we tracking to the approved cost envelope?
- Where are the variances and what is driving them?
- Do we need to update the forecast based on what we know now?
- Are there cost risks that need early escalation?
- Is the run-rate cost in line with the value being delivered?

## Inputs

- Approved budget and investment commitment
- Actuals to date from finance systems
- Delivery progress and remaining scope
- Known changes to scope, team or timeline
- Vendor and contract commitments

## Activities

- Compare actuals against forecast and approved budget
- Update the forecast based on current delivery reality
- Identify and explain material variances
- Flag cost risks or savings opportunities
- Feed updated numbers into portfolio-level reporting

## Outputs and decisions

### Confirm outlook
- Forecast is on track and no action is needed
- Continue with current plan

### Update forecast
- Forecast has changed based on new information
- Updated numbers are shared with portfolio and finance

### Escalate variance
- A material variance has been identified that needs attention
- Escalated to the relevant decision maker with options

### Replan
- The financial plan needs a significant reset
- Triggers a revised business case or scope adjustment conversation

## Who is involved

### Delivery Lead
- owns the delivery-level forecast and flags variances early

### Head of Product
- provides product context for cost changes and supports prioritisation decisions

### Portfolio Partner
- consolidates forecasts across the portfolio and escalates where needed

**Others who may contribute:** Finance Business Partner, Product Owner, Scrum Master.

## How it connects to lifecycle stages and deliverables

Forecasting starts when an investment is approved and continues through delivery and live operation. It draws on the [Delivery Pack](../deliverables/delivery-pack/) for progress context and feeds into [Report Financial Status](../../financial-process.html?p=report-financial-status).

During live operation, forecasting connects to the [Product Review Pack](../deliverables/product-review-pack/) where cost and value are reviewed together.

## Common pitfalls

**Forecasting only at the start**

A forecast that is never updated becomes fiction. Build forecasting into your regular delivery rhythm.

**Hiding variances until they are too large to manage**

Small variances are normal. The problem is when they are not surfaced early enough to act on. Make variance visibility a habit, not an escalation.

**Disconnecting cost tracking from delivery progress**

If the financial view and the delivery view live in separate conversations, neither is reliable. Keep them connected.

**Treating forecasting as a finance team exercise**

The delivery team knows what is happening on the ground. Forecasting works best when the people closest to the work own the numbers.
