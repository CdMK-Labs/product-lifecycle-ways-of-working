## What this deliverable is for

The Delivery Pack is the practical deliverable used to support execution once a topic has been approved and is ready to move into implementation.

Its purpose is to bring the most important execution information together in one place: what is being delivered, how the work is progressing, which dependencies and issues matter, what has been validated, and what still needs to be resolved before deployment.

The Delivery Pack is not meant to replace delivery tools or become a detailed project record. It should give the team and stakeholders a clear and usable view of execution, while linking to source tools and evidence where appropriate.

A good Delivery Pack helps the team keep work visible, manage execution responsibly, and build quality, security and operational readiness into the work as it progresses.

The Delivery Pack should be maintained throughout execution and reviewed at a cadence that fits the work. For some teams, that may follow sprint or iteration rhythms. For others, it may follow milestones, release checkpoints or dependency reviews. The important point is that it stays current enough to support coordination, decisions and deployment readiness.

## When to use it

Use this deliverable in [Develop & Deliver](stage.html?s=develop-and-deliver).

It should be used when:
- the required approvals and commitments are in place
- the team is moving from planning into execution
- scope, priorities and dependencies need to stay visible during the work
- validation, quality and operational readiness need to be tracked as part of delivery
- the team needs a clear baseline for readiness before deployment

For larger initiatives, the Delivery Pack may support milestone reviews, governance follow-up and dependency coordination.

For smaller product changes, it can still be used in a lighter way to make execution visible and disciplined without creating unnecessary overhead.

## Who is typically involved

### IT Delivery Lead
Usually co-owns the Delivery Pack and helps keep progress, dependencies, issues and readiness visible throughout execution.

### Product Owner
Usually co-owns the Delivery Pack and helps keep scope, priorities and day-to-day product decisions aligned with the approved intent.

### Head of Product
Helps ensure the execution stays aligned with product outcomes, lifecycle responsibility and responsible trade-offs.

### IT Specialists / Developers
Contribute the practical execution view, implementation details, validation evidence and technical notes needed for delivery and readiness.

### Product Architect
Helps ensure technical integrity, non-functional quality, enabling needs and architectural considerations are handled during execution.

### Adoption Specialist
Contributes where user validation, readiness, training needs or business-facing change implications matter during delivery.

**Others who may contribute:** Business stakeholders, vendors, support roles, dependent teams, Product Manager where larger scope or trade-offs need visibility.

## Minimum contents

The Delivery Pack should create a clear and current view of execution. It should stay practical and close to the work, rather than turning into a second planning document.

### 1. Title
A clear and simple name for the delivery effort or change.

### 2. Current scope and priorities
A short view of what is currently being delivered and what matters most right now.

This section should make it clear what the team is actively working toward.

### 3. Work breakdown, backlog, flow or milestones
A view of how the work is structured and tracked during execution.

This may be shown through backlog items, milestones, flow items, release slices or another fit-for-purpose structure.

### 4. Dependencies and issues
A summary of the dependencies and issues that matter most for execution.

This section should focus on what could slow progress, create risk or require active coordination.

### 5. Key implementation decisions
A short record of the most important delivery decisions made during execution.

This helps keep the reasoning visible when trade-offs, clarifications or adjustments are made.

### 6. Quality and test evidence
A summary of how quality is being validated during delivery.

This may include testing, review evidence, demos, stakeholder validation or other relevant quality checks.

### 7. Security and compliance checks
A short view of the relevant security, compliance and non-functional checks that are being handled during execution.

This section helps ensure these concerns are built in, not left until the end.

### 8. Technical or configuration notes
A short summary of the technical or configuration information that matters for understanding the delivered change and preparing for deployment.

This section should link to source material where appropriate.

### 9. Validation or demo outcomes
A summary of the most important outcomes from user validation, stakeholder review, demonstrations or similar checkpoints.

This section should help show whether the work is moving in the right direction.

### 10. Open items before deployment
A clear view of what still needs to be completed, resolved or confirmed before the change is ready to move into Deploy.

This section is important for release readiness.

### 11. Material changes requiring replanning
A short view of any major changes in scope, assumptions, dependencies or risks that may require the team to realign with the earlier plan or return to a planning discussion.

This should help avoid drifting too far from the approved intent without visibility.

### 12. Owner and contributors
The people responsible for the pack and the main contributors involved.

## What good looks like

A good Delivery Pack is current, practical and easy to use.

It should:
- make execution visible without becoming heavy
- show what is being delivered and how progress is being managed
- surface the dependencies and issues that really matter
- make quality, security and validation visible as part of the work
- keep operationally relevant information close to the delivery
- make readiness for deployment easier to assess
- support adjustment where needed without losing control of the commitment

A strong Delivery Pack helps the team move with pace while still maintaining discipline, transparency and readiness.

## Common pitfalls

**Treating the pack as a second project plan**

The Delivery Pack should support execution, not recreate all planning material in another format.

**Letting the pack drift away from the real work**

If it is not kept close to delivery tools, decisions and evidence, it becomes stale quickly.

**Focusing only on progress and not on readiness**

Execution is not only about getting work done. It is also about building toward a safe and usable deployment.

**Leaving quality, security and documentation until late**

These should be built into the work as it progresses, not treated as end-stage tasks.

**Hiding changes in scope or assumptions**

If material changes emerge during execution, they should be made visible and handled responsibly.

**Capturing too much detail**

The pack should surface what matters, not reproduce every task, note or ticket.

## Related lifecycle stages

This deliverable is most closely linked to [Develop & Deliver](stage.html?s=develop-and-deliver), where approved scope is turned into working outcomes.

It builds on:
- [Align & Plan](stage.html?s=align-and-plan), which defines the approved direction, scope, estimates and delivery setup

It leads into:
- [Deploy](stage.html?s=deploy), where the change is released, rolled out and made ready for live use

In that sense, the Delivery Pack is the working bridge between commitment and release.

## Templates and supporting materials

The core template for this deliverable is the [Delivery Pack template](supporting-materials/delivery-pack/).

Supporting materials may include:
- backlog or work-item tool views
- milestone or flow tracking
- dependency logs
- issue logs
- technical notes
- test evidence
- review or demo notes
- security and compliance records
- deployment preparation notes

Where possible, the pack should link to source tools and evidence rather than duplicate detailed delivery information.

The aim is not to create a large execution report. It is to keep the delivery visible, disciplined and ready for deployment.
