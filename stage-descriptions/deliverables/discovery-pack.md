## What this deliverable is for

The Discovery Pack is the deliverable used to capture what was learned before a topic moves into commitment and planning.

Its purpose is to bring together the key outputs from discovery in one place: what problem was explored, what was learned about users, stakeholders and context, which options were considered, what evidence was gathered, and which risks, dependencies and constraints became visible.

The Discovery Pack is not a business case, delivery plan or final solution definition. It is the structured output of discovery and the basis for deciding whether the topic is ready to move into Align & Plan, should be reframed, should be held, or should stop.

A good Discovery Pack helps teams reduce uncertainty without creating unnecessary documentation.

## When to use it

Use this deliverable in [Product Discovery](stage.html?s=product-discovery).

It should be used when:
- an Opportunity Brief or similar value proposition has been prioritised for further exploration
- the topic is important enough to investigate, but not clear enough yet for commitment
- stakeholders need a better understanding of users, options, risks, dependencies or constraints
- the team needs to decide whether the topic is ready to move into planning and approval

For larger topics, the Discovery Pack helps build the bridge between early shaping and formal commitment.

For smaller topics, it can still be used in a lighter way to test assumptions, improve understanding and support a more confident next-step decision.

## Who is typically involved

### Product Manager
Usually leads the overall shaping of the Discovery Pack and ensures that the learning is connected to value, priorities and possible next steps.

### Product Owner
Contributes practical product context, stakeholder understanding and helps define what needs to be learned through discovery.

### Head of Product
Helps assess product relevance, lifecycle implications and whether the topic is mature enough to move forward.

### Business Owner
Provides decision context and helps validate whether the discovery is addressing the right business need.

### Adoption Specialist
Helps bring user, adoption and behavioral perspectives into the discovery, especially where user needs, readiness or friction matter.

### Product Architect
Helps surface architectural constraints, non-functional implications, enabling needs and major technical risks.

**Others who may contribute:** IT Value Partner, IT Architecture Partner, IT Specialists / Developers, relevant business stakeholders, super users, operational representatives.

## Minimum contents

The Discovery Pack should document what was learned and what that means for the next decision. It should stay focused on reducing uncertainty, not drift into detailed planning.

### 1. Title
A clear and simple name for the topic under discovery.

### 2. Discovery summary
A short summary of what was explored, what was learned and what is being recommended.

This section should make it easy for a reader to understand the result of the discovery quickly.

### 3. Refined problem statement
A clearer version of the problem or opportunity after discovery work has been done.

This should show how the team's understanding improved during the stage.

### 4. Users and stakeholders
A short description of the users, teams, business stakeholders or operational groups that matter most to the topic.

This section should make visible who is affected and whose perspective was considered.

### 5. Key insights and evidence
A summary of the most important evidence gathered during discovery.

This may come from interviews, observations, feedback, usage insight, comparable cases, operational input or other relevant sources.

### 6. Assumptions tested
A short view of the main assumptions explored during discovery and what was learned from them.

This section should help show where uncertainty has been reduced and where it remains.

### 7. Options explored
A summary of the main options or directions considered.

This section should focus on the relevant trade-offs, not on creating a polished solution proposal too early.

### 8. Prototype or experiment summary
A short summary of any lightweight prototype, mock-up or experiment used to learn faster.

This should explain what was tested and what was learned, not present the prototype as a committed solution.

### 9. Risks, dependencies and constraints
A summary of the main risks, dependencies, architectural constraints, security concerns, operational implications or other factors that could affect the topic later.

This section should make important constraints visible early.

### 10. Lifecycle considerations
A short view of what discovery revealed about adoption, operations, architecture, security, sustainability or other lifecycle concerns.

This section helps connect discovery to full lifecycle responsibility rather than only feature thinking.

### 11. Competency and capacity implications
A first view of the competencies likely to be needed and any important capacity implications if the topic moves forward.

This does not need detailed staffing or estimation, but it should highlight important implications for later planning.

### 12. Recommendation
A clear recommendation on next step:
- move into Align & Plan
- return for reframing
- hold
- stop

The reader should never be left unsure what is being recommended.

### 13. Owner and contributors
The person responsible for the pack and the main contributors involved.

## What good looks like

A good Discovery Pack is focused, evidence-based and honest about uncertainty.

It should:
- show that the problem is better understood than before discovery started
- make the most important users, stakeholders and affected areas visible
- capture the strongest evidence and insights without drowning the reader in detail
- show that meaningful options and trade-offs have been explored
- surface the major risks, dependencies and constraints early
- make lifecycle implications visible before commitment
- create enough clarity to support a decision on whether the topic should move forward

A strong Discovery Pack gives decision-makers and product teams a better basis for commitment, while still being light enough to produce quickly and iteratively.

## Common pitfalls

**Treating discovery as solution validation only**

If the pack only justifies a preferred idea rather than showing what was actually learned, discovery becomes weaker.

**Drifting into detailed planning**

The Discovery Pack should reduce uncertainty, not become a business case or delivery plan.

**Documenting activity rather than insight**

A long list of interviews, workshops or prototypes is not useful unless it clearly shows what was learned.

**Ignoring user or operational perspectives**

Discovery becomes weaker when it reflects only stakeholder opinion and misses user, product or operational realities.

**Hiding uncertainty to make the topic look ready**

A good Discovery Pack should make remaining uncertainty visible, not pretend it has disappeared.

**Treating prototypes as committed solutions**

Prototypes and experiments should support learning, not lock the team into an answer too early.

## Related lifecycle stages

This deliverable is most closely linked to [Product Discovery](stage.html?s=product-discovery), where the purpose is to build understanding before moving into commitment.

It builds on:
- [Value Proposition](stage.html?s=value-proposition), which clarifies why the topic matters and whether it is worth exploring further

It can lead into:
- [Align & Plan](stage.html?s=align-and-plan), when the discovery has created enough clarity for planning, approval and commitment
- or back into reframing, holding or stopping if the discovery shows that the topic is not yet ready or should not move forward

In that sense, the Discovery Pack is the bridge between early shaping and commitment.

## Templates and supporting materials

The core template for this deliverable is the [Discovery Pack template](supporting-materials/discovery-pack/).

Supporting materials may include:
- interview or research notes
- lightweight stakeholder maps
- assumptions logs
- option comparisons
- prototype or experiment summaries
- references to related discoveries or earlier learnings

Where possible, the pack should link to source material rather than duplicate all supporting evidence.

The aim is not to create a polished report. It is to create enough insight and clarity to support the next decision.
