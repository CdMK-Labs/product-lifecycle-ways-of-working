## What this pack is for

The Discovery Pack captures what was learned before a topic moves into commitment and planning. Its purpose is to bring the key discovery outputs together in one place and support a clear next-step decision: move into Align & Plan, return for reframing, hold or stop.

It is not a business case or delivery plan. It should reduce uncertainty, not simulate commitment.

## What's in this pack

#### [Discovery Pack template](supporting-materials/discovery-pack/)
Your working document for capturing problem understanding, insights, options explored, risks and the recommendation on next steps.

Supporting materials that can be linked from the pack:
- interview or research notes
- lightweight stakeholder maps
- assumptions logs
- option comparisons
- prototype or experiment summaries

## When to use this pack

Use this pack during [Product Discovery](stage.html?s=product-discovery).

The trigger is typically:
- an Opportunity Brief has been prioritised for further exploration
- the topic is important enough to investigate but not yet clear enough for commitment
- stakeholders need a better understanding of users, options, risks or dependencies
- the team needs to test assumptions before moving into planning

For larger topics, the pack helps build the bridge between early shaping and formal commitment. For smaller topics, it can still be used in a lighter way to improve understanding and support a more confident decision.

## Minimum viable starting point

A refined problem statement, a short summary of the main insights and options explored, a view of the biggest risks or dependencies, and a clear recommendation is enough to move the conversation forward.

Not every section needs to be detailed. Fill out what was actually explored and leave sections brief where discovery was lighter. The goal is honest insight, not a polished report.

## Pack contents

The pack should document what was learned and what that means for the next decision. Stay focused on reducing uncertainty, not drifting into planning.

### 1. Title
A clear and simple name for the topic under discovery.

### 2. Discovery summary
A short summary of what was explored, what was learned and what is being recommended. Should be easy to understand without reading the full pack.

### 3. Refined problem statement
A clearer version of the problem or opportunity after discovery work. This should show how understanding improved during the stage.

### 4. Users and stakeholders
The users, teams, business stakeholders or operational groups that matter most to the topic, and whose perspective was considered.

### 5. Key insights and evidence
The most important evidence gathered: interviews, observations, feedback, usage insight, comparable cases, operational input or other relevant sources.

### 6. Assumptions tested
The main assumptions explored during discovery and what was learned from them. Should show where uncertainty has been reduced and where it remains.

### 7. Options explored
The main options or directions considered, with the relevant trade-offs. Not a polished solution proposal.

### 8. Prototype or experiment summary
A short summary of any lightweight prototype, mock-up or experiment used to learn faster: what was tested and what was learned. Not a commitment to an answer.

### 9. Risks, dependencies and constraints
The main risks, dependencies, architectural constraints, security concerns, operational implications or other factors that could affect the topic later.

### 10. Lifecycle considerations
What discovery revealed about adoption, operations, architecture, security, sustainability or other lifecycle concerns.

### 11. Competency and capacity implications
A first view of the competencies likely needed and any important capacity implications if the topic moves forward. Not detailed staffing, but visible enough for planning.

### 12. Recommendation
Clear next step: move into Align & Plan, return for reframing, hold or stop.

### 13. Owner and contributors
The person responsible for the pack and the main contributors involved.

## Who is typically involved

**Product Manager** — usually leads the overall shaping and connects the learning to value and next steps.

**Product Owner** — contributes practical product context, stakeholder understanding and helps define what needs to be learned.

**Head of Product** — helps assess product relevance, lifecycle implications and whether the topic is mature enough to move forward.

**Business Owner** — validates whether the discovery is addressing the right business need.

**Adoption Specialist** — brings user, adoption and behavioral perspectives into the discovery.

**Product Architect** — surfaces architectural constraints, non-functional implications and major technical risks.

**Others who may contribute:** IT Value Partner, IT Architecture Partner, IT Specialists / Developers, business stakeholders, super users, operational representatives.

## What good looks like

A good Discovery Pack is focused, evidence-based and honest about uncertainty. It:
- shows that the problem is better understood than before discovery started
- captures the strongest insights without drowning the reader in detail
- surfaces the major risks, dependencies and constraints early
- makes lifecycle implications visible before commitment
- creates enough clarity to support a decision on whether to move forward

A strong pack gives decision-makers a better basis for commitment while still being light enough to produce quickly.

## Common pitfalls

**Treating discovery as solution validation only**

If the pack only justifies a preferred idea rather than showing what was actually learned, discovery becomes weaker.

**Drifting into detailed planning**

The Discovery Pack should reduce uncertainty, not become a business case or delivery plan.

**Documenting activity rather than insight**

A long list of interviews or workshops is not useful unless it clearly shows what was learned.

**Hiding uncertainty to make the topic look ready**

A good Discovery Pack makes remaining uncertainty visible. It should not pretend it has disappeared.

**Treating prototypes as committed solutions**

Prototypes and experiments should support learning, not lock the team into an answer too early.
