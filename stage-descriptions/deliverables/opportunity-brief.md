## What this deliverable is for

The Opportunity Brief is the lightweight deliverable used to clarify why a topic is worth attention before more time, capacity or funding is assigned.

Its purpose is to capture the opportunity in a simple and decision-oriented way: what the need is, why it matters now, what value could be created, how it relates to business and product priorities, and whether it should move forward.

The Opportunity Brief is not a business case, delivery plan or solution design. It is the first structured step in turning a need or idea into something that can be prioritised, discussed and, if relevant, moved into discovery.

A good Opportunity Brief creates just enough clarity to support a next-step decision without creating unnecessary documentation.

## When to use it

Use this deliverable in [Value Proposition](stage.html?s=value-proposition).

It should be used when:
- a new business need, opportunity or problem has been identified
- an existing product needs further investment to create value, reduce risk or improve sustainability
- stakeholders need a shared view of why something matters before assigning more capacity or funding
- the team needs to decide whether the topic should move forward, be held, be reshaped or stop

For larger topics, the Opportunity Brief can help frame an investment candidate before discovery and later approval work.

For smaller product changes, it can help clarify whether the topic belongs in normal product flow, should be prioritised later, or needs broader shaping before moving on.

## Who is typically involved

### Product Manager
Often leads the shaping of the Opportunity Brief and connects the topic to business priorities, portfolio direction and investment dialogue.

### IT Value Partner
Often plays a central role when the topic is not yet clearly anchored in an existing product or needs early value shaping across stakeholders.

### Business Owner
Provides business context, explains why the topic matters and helps judge whether it is worth taking forward.

### Head of Product
Contributes when the opportunity relates to an existing product and helps assess product relevance, lifecycle fit and likely implications.

### Product Owner
Can contribute product context, stakeholder insight and understanding of current product needs where relevant.

**Others who may contribute:** Product Architect, IT Architecture Partner, IT Portfolio Partner, Adoption Specialist, relevant business stakeholders.

## Minimum contents

The Opportunity Brief should stay short and focused. It should create enough clarity to support a next-step decision, not try to answer everything upfront.

### 1. Title
A clear and simple name for the opportunity.

### 2. Short summary
A short description of the opportunity in plain language. This should be understandable to someone not already close to the topic.

### 3. Problem or opportunity
A clear description of the need, issue or opportunity being raised. This section should focus on the real need, not an assumed solution.

### 4. Why now
A short explanation of why the topic matters now. This could be driven by business timing, risk, product health, compliance, operational pain, sustainability concerns or a strategic opportunity.

### 5. Affected area
A short description of who is affected and where the impact sits. This could include users, teams, processes, business functions, markets or product areas.

### 6. Expected value
A high-level statement of the value this could create or protect. The value may relate to business impact, efficiency, quality, resilience, compliance, technical debt reduction, simplification or sustainability.

### 7. Strategic fit
A short explanation of how the topic connects to business priorities, product direction or broader strategic intent.

### 8. Primary rationale for investment
A clear statement of the main reason this should be considered.

For example:
- business value
- risk reduction
- compliance or resilience
- technical debt or lifecycle sustainability

### 9. Theme or sunray or investment candidate
A simple classification of the topic at its current maturity. This should help frame the discussion without forcing the topic too quickly into detailed scope or solutioning.

### 10. Initial funding frame view
A first view of whether the topic is likely to stay within normal product flow, fit within an existing investment frame, or require a new investment path. This does not need detailed financials, but it should help orient the next conversation.

### 11. Recommendation
A clear recommendation on next step: move forward, hold, reshape or stop. The reader should never be left unsure what is being recommended.

### 12. Owner and contributors
The person responsible for the brief and the main contributors involved.

## What good looks like

A good Opportunity Brief is short, clear and useful.

It should:
- explain the need in plain language
- make the expected value visible at a high level
- show why the topic matters now
- connect the topic to business or product priorities
- give enough context to support prioritisation and next-step discussion
- stay light enough that it does not become a mini business case
- end with a clear recommendation

A strong Opportunity Brief helps the right people decide whether the topic should move into discovery, stay on hold, be reframed or stop.

## Common pitfalls

**Jumping too quickly to solution ideas**

If the brief starts by describing what to build rather than what problem matters, the discussion becomes narrower too early.

**Making the value too vague**

If the expected value is unclear, the topic becomes difficult to prioritise.

**Treating every topic as a major investment candidate**

Some topics belong in normal product flow, and some should not move forward at all.

**Trying to answer everything too early**

The Opportunity Brief should support early clarity, not become a full business case, delivery plan or discovery report.

**Ignoring product health and lifecycle needs**

Not all valuable investments are about new business features. Some are about resilience, simplification, sustainability, compliance or technical debt.

**Working without the right business context**

A weak brief often comes from unclear ownership or weak connection to real priorities.

## Related lifecycle stages

This deliverable is most closely linked to [Value Proposition](stage.html?s=value-proposition), where the purpose is to clarify why the topic matters and whether it should move forward.

It can lead into:
- [Product Discovery](stage.html?s=product-discovery), when the topic needs deeper understanding before commitment
- [Align & Plan](stage.html?s=align-and-plan), indirectly, once discovery has created enough clarity to shape the case further

It may also lead to a decision to keep the topic in normal product flow, hold it for later prioritisation, reshape it or stop it.

In that sense, the Opportunity Brief is the starting point for early shaping.

## Templates and supporting materials

The core template for this deliverable is the [Opportunity Brief template](supporting-materials/opportunity-brief/).

Supporting materials may include:
- lightweight stakeholder mapping
- links to relevant product or business context
- related themes or sunrays
- notes from initial discussions, where useful

Where possible, the brief should link to source material rather than repeat information that already exists elsewhere.

The aim is not to create a polished case. It is to create enough clarity to support the next decision.
