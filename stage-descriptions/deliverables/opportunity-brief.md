## What this pack is for

The Opportunity Brief captures why a topic is worth attention before more time or funding is assigned. It is the first structured step in deciding whether something should move forward, be held, be reshaped or stop.

It is not a business case, delivery plan or solution design. Its job is to create just enough clarity to support a next-step decision.

## What's in this pack

#### [Opportunity Brief template](supporting-materials/opportunity-brief/)
Your working document for framing the problem, expected value, strategic fit and next-step recommendation.

Supporting materials that can be linked from the brief:
- lightweight stakeholder mapping
- related themes or sunrays
- links to relevant business or product context
- notes from initial discussions, where useful

## When to use this pack

Use this pack during [Value Proposition](stage.html?s=value-proposition).

The trigger is typically:
- a new business need, opportunity or problem has been raised
- an existing product needs further investment and needs a clear framing before the conversation moves forward
- stakeholders need a shared view of why something matters before capacity or funding is assigned
- a portfolio or prioritisation conversation is coming up and the topic needs to be articulated clearly

For larger topics, the brief helps frame an investment candidate before discovery and approval work begins. For smaller changes, it helps confirm whether the topic belongs in normal product flow or needs broader shaping first.

## Minimum viable starting point

You do not need all sections to open a useful conversation. A clear problem description, a short value statement, a first view of strategic fit and a recommendation is enough to get started.

Add the remaining sections as the conversation matures or as the topic becomes more complex. The goal is enough clarity to make a next-step decision, not a polished document.

## Pack contents

The brief should stay short and focused. These sections give structure to the conversation.

### 1. Title
A clear and simple name for the opportunity.

### 2. Short summary
A short description in plain language, understandable to someone not already close to the topic.

### 3. Problem or opportunity
What the need, issue or opportunity is. Focus on the real need, not an assumed solution.

### 4. Why now
Why the topic matters now: business timing, risk, product health, compliance, operational pain, sustainability or a strategic opportunity.

### 5. Affected area
Who is affected and where the impact sits: users, teams, processes, business functions, markets or product areas.

### 6. Expected value
A high-level statement of the value this could create or protect: business impact, efficiency, quality, resilience, compliance, technical debt reduction, simplification or sustainability.

### 7. Strategic fit
How the topic connects to business priorities, product direction or broader strategic intent.

### 8. Primary rationale for investment
The main reason this should be considered: business value, risk reduction, compliance or resilience, or technical debt and lifecycle sustainability.

### 9. Theme, sunray or investment candidate
A simple classification of the topic at its current maturity. This should help frame the discussion without forcing the topic too quickly into scope or solutioning.

### 10. Initial funding frame view
A first view of whether the topic is likely to stay within normal product flow, fit within an existing investment frame, or need a new investment path. No detailed financials needed at this stage.

### 11. Recommendation
A clear recommendation on next step: move forward, hold, reshape or stop. The reader should never be left unsure what is being recommended.

### 12. Owner and contributors
The person responsible for the brief and the main contributors involved.

## Who is typically involved

**Product Manager** — often leads the shaping and connects the topic to business priorities and portfolio direction.

**IT Value Partner** — plays a central role when the topic is not yet clearly anchored in an existing product.

**Business Owner** — provides business context and helps judge whether it is worth taking forward.

**Head of Product** — contributes when the opportunity relates to an existing product.

**Others who may contribute:** Product Owner, Product Architect, IT Architecture Partner, IT Portfolio Partner, Adoption Specialist.

## What good looks like

A good Opportunity Brief is short, clear and useful. It:
- explains the need in plain language
- makes the expected value visible at a high level
- shows why the topic matters now
- stays light enough that it does not become a mini business case
- ends with a clear recommendation

A strong brief helps the right people decide whether the topic should move into discovery, stay on hold, be reframed or stop.

## Common pitfalls

**Jumping too quickly to solution ideas**

If the brief starts by describing what to build rather than what problem matters, the discussion narrows too early.

**Making the value too vague**

If the expected value is unclear, the topic becomes difficult to prioritise.

**Treating every topic as a major investment candidate**

Some topics belong in normal product flow, and some should not move forward at all.

**Trying to answer everything too early**

The brief should support early clarity, not become a full business case, delivery plan or discovery report.

**Ignoring product health and lifecycle needs**

Not all valuable investments are about new business features. Some are about resilience, simplification, compliance or technical debt.
