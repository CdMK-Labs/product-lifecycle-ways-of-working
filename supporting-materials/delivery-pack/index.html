Delivery Pack

What this pack is for

The Delivery Pack is the working deliverable used to keep execution visible and manageable once a topic has been approved and is ready to move into implementation. It brings the most important execution information together in one place: what is being delivered, how the work is progressing, which dependencies and issues matter, what has been validated, and what needs to be resolved before deployment.

It is a working tool, not a status report. Its job is to keep scope, progress, quality and readiness clear for the team throughout execution.

What's in this pack

The pack tracks the practical execution work. It should stay close to the real work and be updated throughout delivery, not rebuilt from scratch at key moments.

Current scope and priorities

What is currently being delivered and what matters most right now. Should reflect the approved scope and any agreed adjustments as execution progresses.

Work breakdown, backlog, flow or milestones

How the work is structured and tracked: backlog items, milestones, flow items, release slices or another fit-for-purpose structure. Link to the source tool rather than duplicating.

Dependencies and issues

The dependencies and issues that matter most for execution: what could slow progress, create risk or require active coordination across teams.

Key implementation decisions

The most important delivery decisions made during execution. Keeps the reasoning visible when trade-offs, clarifications or adjustments are made mid-delivery.

Quality and test evidence

How quality is being validated: testing, review evidence, demos, stakeholder validation or other relevant quality checks. Should make quality visible as part of the work, not as an end-stage task.

Security and compliance checks

The relevant security, compliance and non-functional checks being handled during execution. These should be built in throughout delivery, not deferred to the end.

Technical or configuration notes

Technical or configuration information that matters for understanding the delivered change and preparing for deployment. Link to source material where appropriate.

Validation or demo outcomes

The most important outcomes from user validation, stakeholder review, demonstrations or similar checkpoints during delivery.

Open items before deployment

What still needs to be completed, resolved or confirmed before the change is ready to move into Deploy. This section is the clearest signal of deployment readiness.

Material changes requiring replanning

Any major changes in scope, assumptions, dependencies or risks that may require the team to realign with the earlier plan or return to a planning discussion.

When to use this pack

Use this pack in Develop & Deliver, once the required approvals and commitments are in place and the team is moving from planning into execution.

The trigger is typically:

  • the Business Case & Delivery Decision Pack has been approved and the team is ready to build
  • scope, priorities and dependencies need to stay visible during the work
  • quality and operational readiness need to be tracked as part of delivery
  • the team needs a shared view of what is left before deployment

For larger initiatives, the pack may support milestone reviews, governance follow-up and dependency coordination. For smaller changes, it can be used more lightly to make execution visible without unnecessary overhead.

This pack is not the right tool for:

  • detailed project planning or Gantt-style scheduling — those belong in delivery tooling
  • replanning the approved scope — use it to flag material changes and trigger a proper realignment
  • deployment coordination and go-live readiness — that is the job of the Release & Readiness Pack

Minimum viable starting point

A current scope view, a short dependency and issues summary, and a clear view of what still needs to be done before deployment is enough to keep execution visible. At minimum the pack should show:

  • what is currently in scope and what the team is focused on
  • the dependencies and issues that need active attention
  • how quality and validation are being handled
  • what is still open before deployment can begin

Start lean and add sections as the work progresses and as the team identifies what needs tracking. The pack should reflect the real work, not become a second planning document.

What good looks like

A good Delivery Pack:

  • stays current and close to the real delivery work rather than becoming a formal reporting artifact
  • makes execution visible without creating unnecessary overhead for the team
  • surfaces the dependencies and issues that actually matter for pace and readiness
  • makes quality, security and validation visible throughout delivery, not only at the end
  • keeps operationally relevant information close to the delivery so the handoff to Deploy is easier
  • makes readiness for deployment genuinely assessable at any point

How to work with this pack in practice

  • Keep the pack close to your delivery tools. Link to backlogs, issue trackers, test evidence and dependency logs rather than copying them in.
  • Update the pack at the cadence that fits the work: sprint reviews, milestones, release checkpoints or dependency coordination moments.
  • Use the open items section actively. It is the clearest signal of whether the team is ready to move to Deploy.
  • When material changes emerge, make them visible in the pack and use the material changes section to flag whether replanning is needed.
  • Quality, security and compliance checks should show up in the pack as the work progresses, not only at the end. If they are absent mid-delivery, that is a signal, not a gap to fill later.
  • The pack should still be useful once delivery is complete. It provides the context for the Release & Readiness Pack and for the team taking on live operations.

Common mistakes to avoid

Treating the pack as a second project plan

The Delivery Pack should support execution, not recreate all planning material in another format. Planning lives in delivery tooling. The pack surfaces what matters for coordination, quality and readiness.

Letting the pack drift away from the real work

If it is not kept close to delivery tools, decisions and evidence, it becomes stale quickly. A stale pack is harder to trust and less useful for deployment readiness assessment.

Focusing only on progress and not on readiness

Execution is not only about getting work done. It is also about building toward a safe and usable deployment. The open items and quality sections exist to keep that visible.

Leaving quality, security and documentation until late

These should be built into the work as it progresses. If they appear only in the final sprint, they are more likely to create pressure, shortcuts or deployment risk.

Hiding material changes in scope or assumptions

If significant changes emerge during execution, they should be surfaced in the pack and handled through a proper replanning conversation. Carrying unacknowledged changes forward creates risk at deployment and in live operation.

Confusing delivery readiness with deployment readiness

Finishing the work is not the same as being ready to deploy. Deployment readiness also depends on support setup, monitoring, communication and operational preparation. Those belong in the Release & Readiness Pack.
