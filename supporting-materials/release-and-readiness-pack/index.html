Release & Readiness Pack

What this pack is for

The Release & Readiness Pack supports safe release, controlled rollout and a smooth move into live operation. It brings the most important release and readiness information together so the team and stakeholders can see whether the change is genuinely ready to release, support and use.

It is not only a technical deployment checklist. A good Release & Readiness Pack helps reduce avoidable instability, improves coordination at go-live and makes the move into normal operation cleaner. The outcome is either a confident move to live, or a clear decision to stabilise, rework or rollback.

What's in this pack

The pack coordinates everything that needs to be in place before and during go-live. It should stay practical and action-oriented, not become a full technical runbook.

Release summary

What is being released, why it matters and what the release is expected to achieve. Sets the context for the rest of the pack.

Deployment plan

How the release will happen: deployment pipelines, automation, manual controls or coordinated release steps. Main release flow, key responsibilities and approval or control points.

Rollout plan

How the change will be introduced to the relevant users, teams, markets or functions. Rollout sequencing and scope where relevant.

Readiness checklist

The most important readiness points that must be confirmed before go-live. Helps the team and stakeholders judge whether the change is truly ready, not just technically deployed.

Support setup

Who will support the product at and after go-live, how support is organized and where issues should go. Weak support setup is one of the most common causes of avoidable instability in the early live period.

Monitoring and alert readiness

The monitoring, alerting and operational visibility that will be in place from day one. Helps ensure the team can see and respond to issues quickly after release.

Access and permissions readiness

Whether the relevant users, roles, teams or support functions will have the access they need at go-live.

Runbooks and support notes

The operational notes, runbooks or support guidance that exist to help with live use and issue handling. Link to the underlying material rather than copying it in.

Communication and training actions

What is being communicated, who needs to be informed, and what training or guidance is in place. These often need to happen before the release, not after.

Rollback and contingency plan

What will happen if the release does not work as expected. Should make major release-risk responses visible without requiring exhaustive scenario planning in the main pack.

Go-live issues and actions

The most important known issues, immediate post-go-live actions and stabilisation items. Going live does not mean all issues are resolved.

Go-live confirmation or decision

Whether the release is approved to proceed, has gone live, is live with conditions, or needs delay, rework or rollback. This should always be explicit.

When to use this pack

Use this pack in Deploy, when the relevant changes are ready to move toward production and the team needs to confirm that deployment, support and user readiness have been handled.

The trigger is typically:

  • quality, security and non-functional checks have reached the level needed for go-live
  • support, monitoring and operational readiness need to be confirmed
  • rollout, communication and training activities need to be coordinated
  • the team needs a clear basis for the go-live decision

The pack should be prepared ahead of release and kept current as readiness evolves. For larger deliveries it may support formal go-live checkpoints and coordinated release governance. For smaller changes it can be used more lightly to confirm that the essentials have been handled responsibly.

This pack is not the right tool for:

  • tracking in-flight delivery work — that belongs in the Delivery Pack
  • detailed technical runbooks — link to those from the pack rather than embedding them
  • post-live product review — that is what the Product Review Pack is for

Minimum viable starting point

A release summary, a clear deployment and rollout plan, a confirmed support and monitoring setup, and a go-live decision statement is enough to proceed for most changes. At minimum the pack should show:

  • what is being released and what the go-live path looks like
  • that the readiness checklist has been worked through
  • that support, monitoring and access are in place
  • what happens if the release needs to be rolled back
  • a clear go-live confirmation or decision

A small internal update may need only a brief readiness checklist and a short support note. A larger rollout needs all sections in full, including rollback thinking and communication actions. Scale the pack to the risk and complexity of the release.

What good looks like

A good Release & Readiness Pack:

  • makes the release scope and go-live path easy to understand for everyone involved
  • shows that technical deployment, support and monitoring have been genuinely prepared
  • makes user and stakeholder readiness visible, not assumed
  • surfaces known risks and contingency actions clearly rather than hiding them
  • supports a controlled move into normal operation rather than a rushed handoff
  • makes good use of automated testing, deployment pipelines and repeatable controls where the product context supports it
  • ends with a clear go-live confirmation or decision — never leaves the status ambiguous

How to work with this pack in practice

  • Start preparing the pack before the final sprint or milestone, not the day before go-live. Many readiness activities need lead time.
  • Link to deployment checklists, runbooks, rollout plans and support documentation rather than reproducing them in the pack. The pack surfaces readiness; the source material carries the detail.
  • Confirm the go-live decision explicitly with the relevant people. Do not assume that completing the checklist means approval to proceed.
  • Treat the communication and training actions as part of readiness, not a follow-up task. Users and stakeholders often need to be prepared before the release, not informed after it.
  • Surface known issues and stabilisation needs honestly. Going live with a list of known issues is fine as long as they are visible and owned. Hiding them creates problems after go-live.
  • After go-live, keep the pack current with confirmed outcomes and any post-live actions until the product has settled into normal operation. It feeds the initial handoff into Operate & Monitor.

Common mistakes to avoid

Treating release as only a technical deployment

A good release also depends on rollout, support readiness, communication and user preparation. If only the deployment pipeline is confirmed, the risk of early-live instability and confusion is higher.

Going live without clear operational readiness

If support setup, monitoring, access or runbooks are weak at go-live, avoidable instability is created from day one. The operations team inherits whatever readiness state exists at handoff.

Leaving adoption activities too late

Users and stakeholders often need communication, guidance or training before the release happens. If this is treated as a post-go-live task, adoption suffers from the start.

Assuming live means finished

Known issues, stabilisation needs and immediate follow-up should still be visible after go-live. Going live transitions responsibility, it does not close all open items.

Unclear ownership during go-live

If responsibilities are blurred at go-live, issue handling and coordination become weaker exactly when pressure is highest. The pack should make it clear who owns what during and immediately after release.

Not preparing a rollback plan

For significant releases, the absence of a rollback plan is a risk in itself. Even if rollback is unlikely, the plan should exist and be understood before go-live.
