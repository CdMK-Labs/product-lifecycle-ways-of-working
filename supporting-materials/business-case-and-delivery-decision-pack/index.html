Business Case & Delivery Decision Pack

What this pack is for

The Business Case & Delivery Decision Pack is the approval deliverable for the Development stage. It brings together value case, cost case and delivery plan so decision-makers can commit with confidence, and the delivery team has a clear baseline for execution.

It is the right deliverable when a topic has moved beyond discovery and needs formal commitment or sign-off before significant execution begins.

What's in this pack

Main pack

The decision narrative. Contains the opportunity and outcome, value case, scope and approach, delivery setup, timeline and milestones, cost summary, key risks and an explicit decision requested. This is what decision-makers read. Keep it focused on the decision, not the detail.

Appendices

Appendices carry the supporting detail. They sit outside the main narrative so the main pack stays readable. Include only the appendices the decision genuinely requires. Not every commitment needs all four in full.

KPI Appendix

Defines leading and lagging KPIs, how success will be measured, ownership and reporting cadence. Establishes the measurement baseline the team will use once delivery is underway.

Architecture Appendix

Describes the high-level technical direction, key integrations, non-functional requirements and major architectural constraints. Supports the delivery setup section in the main pack without overloading it.

Estimates & Financial Appendix

Documents expected development cost, run-cost implications, key assumptions and contingency. Provides the financial detail behind the cost summary in the main pack.

Risk Appendix

Makes major risks, mitigation actions, dependencies and confidence level visible. Supports the risk summary in the main pack and gives reviewers the full risk picture if they need it.

When to use this pack

Use this pack in Align & Plan, when Product Discovery has created enough clarity to move from exploration into commitment.

It supports:

  • the approval decision for larger initiatives requiring formal financial commitment
  • the commitment conversation for smaller product changes within normal product or portfolio governance
  • alignment between the business owner, product team and delivery team before execution starts

The pack scales with the size of the commitment. A large initiative needs all four appendices developed in full. A smaller change may need only brief appendix notes, or none at all, as long as the main pack decision narrative is clear.

Minimum viable starting point

The main pack should be readable without the appendices. At minimum it should contain:

  • why this matters now and what outcome is expected
  • what is in scope and what is out of scope
  • the delivery approach and major milestones
  • a cost and funding summary at decision level
  • key risks and dependencies the decision-maker needs to know
  • an explicit recommendation and decision requested

Start with the main pack. Add appendix depth only where the decision genuinely requires it. A clear, honest main pack with limited appendices is a stronger starting point than a long compilation of detail without a coherent narrative.

What good looks like

A good Business Case & Delivery Decision Pack:

  • can be read quickly by a decision-maker who was not in every shaping conversation
  • connects value, cost and delivery in a way that feels coherent, not like three separate documents joined together
  • keeps the main pack focused on the decision, not the detail
  • is honest about what is known, what is estimated and what is uncertain
  • gives the team a usable baseline for execution once approved, not just a deliverable to file
  • makes it possible to approve, challenge, reshape or stop the case with confidence

How to work with this pack in practice

  • Start with the main pack narrative. Only add appendix depth when it genuinely improves the decision.
  • Keep the main pack at decision level. Move supporting analysis into appendices rather than expanding the main narrative.
  • Link to source material where it already exists: architecture diagrams, cost models, RAID logs, delivery trackers. Do not copy the same information into the pack.
  • Update the pack as shaping matures rather than creating a new version from scratch each time.
  • Use the pack to prepare for the approval conversation. It is a decision tool, not a documentation exercise.
  • Treat the signed-off pack as the delivery team's working baseline. It should still be useful once the meeting is over.

Common mistakes to avoid

Turning the pack into a large compilation of disconnected material

The pack should be readable and decision-oriented. If it has grown into a stack of slides, notes and spreadsheets without a coherent narrative, it has lost its purpose.

Putting too much detail in the main pack

Detail belongs in appendices. The main pack should surface the decision, not the full analysis. If a decision-maker needs to read fifty pages to understand the case, the pack is not working.

Treating all four appendices as mandatory for every commitment

Appendix depth should match the size and nature of the commitment. Not every item needs full KPI frameworks, detailed cost models, architecture diagrams and RAID logs.

Creating false precision too early

Estimates and projections at this stage are based on incomplete information. Hiding uncertainty behind detailed numbers produces weaker decisions than being honest about assumptions and confidence level.

Duplicating material that already lives elsewhere

Architecture diagrams, delivery plans, RAID logs and financial models often already exist in source tools. Copying them into the pack creates two versions to maintain and usually a less accurate one.

A strong value case with a vague delivery plan

Value case, cost case and delivery plan need to be coherent together. A convincing value argument attached to an unrealistic or unclear delivery shape creates a weak decision.

Ignoring lifecycle implications

A case focused only on features and delivery often misses adoption needs, operational run-cost, sustainability implications and long-term product impact. These belong in the decision, not only in delivery.
